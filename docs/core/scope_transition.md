# Scope Transition

## Overview

This document formalizes the concept of **scope transition** within the ARW framework
and translates it into the context-navigation cognitive architecture used in this repository.

Scope transition occurs when a descriptive regime (scope) loses its ordering dominance
because the degrees of freedom it suppresses become dynamically relevant.

Instead of treating instability as failure, ARW interprets it as a **signal that a new
scope is required**.

In the cognitive architecture, scope transition corresponds to **mode switching** or
the emergence of new processing modes.

---

# 1 ARW Definition

Within ARW, a scope stabilizes a system description by suppressing certain degrees
of freedom while explicitly representing others.

As long as suppressed freedoms remain weakly coupled or incoherent, the scope
remains admissible and ordnend dominant.

When suppressed degrees of freedom become systematically relevant:

- admissibility degrades
- scope dominance is lost
- instability or chaos appears.

This situation indicates that the system is operating beyond the stabilizing
capacity of the chosen scope.

A **scope transition** becomes necessary.

---

# 2 Emergence Through Scope Transition

From the ARW perspective, emergence occurs when a new descriptive regime becomes
dominant.

At this higher scope:

- previously latent interactions become explicit
- patterns that appeared chaotic at the lower scope become structured
- the system regains descriptive stability.

Thus emergence does not introduce new dynamics; it reorganizes description around
a new ordering structure.

---

# 3 Translation to Cognitive Architecture

In the context-navigation architecture:

Global scope
→ full observable state space

Processing modes
→ reduced scopes that prioritize subsets of observables

A **scope transition** corresponds to switching between processing modes when the
current mode becomes inadmissible.

---

# 4 Mode Switching

When admissibility decreases for the current mode:

A(m_current | c) ↓

alternative modes are evaluated.

The agent transitions to a new mode whose scope remains compatible with the
currently active degrees of freedom.

Mode selection:

m* = argmax_m A(m | c)

Thus mode switching is the operational form of scope transition.

---

# 5 Triggers of Scope Transition

Scope transitions can be triggered by:

Environmental change
→ new interactions activate previously latent variables

Internal prediction error
→ suppressed observables begin influencing outcomes

Boundary condition shifts
→ new constraints alter interaction structure

Accumulation of latent effects
→ previously negligible freedoms become significant

---

# 6 Role of Salience

Salience increases near scope transition boundaries.

Contexts become salient when:

- multiple modes show similar admissibility
- admissibility changes rapidly with context
- latent degrees of freedom begin activating

Salience therefore acts as a signal that a scope transition may be necessary.

---

# 7 Learning New Scopes

Beyond switching between existing modes, scope transitions can also involve
learning **new processing modes**.

This occurs when no existing mode provides sufficient admissibility.

During offline consolidation the agent may:

- reorganize anchor memory
- discover new clusters of contexts
- construct new reduced scopes that stabilize previously unstable situations.

---

# 8 Structural Interpretation

Scope transitions reflect structural adaptation rather than arbitrary switching.

They occur when:

- the current descriptive regime becomes structurally inadequate
- the environment activates previously suppressed freedoms
- a new regime becomes necessary to restore stability.

Thus scope transitions represent the mechanism through which the agent adapts
its descriptive structure to changing environments.

---

# 9 Conceptual Summary

Scope transition describes the shift from one descriptive regime to another
when the current scope loses its ordering dominance.

In the cognitive architecture:

Scope transition = processing mode transition.

Triggers include:

- admissibility degradation
- activation of latent degrees of freedom
- boundary condition changes
- increased salience.

This mechanism allows the agent to maintain structural compatibility between
its internal models and the evolving environment.
