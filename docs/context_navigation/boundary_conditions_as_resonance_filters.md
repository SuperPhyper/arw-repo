# Boundary Conditions as Resonance Filters

## Overview

This document extracts the ARW concept of **boundary conditions as resonance filters**
and translates it into the cognitive architecture used in this repository.

Within ARW, boundary conditions are not merely constraints on a system.
They determine **which interactions can accumulate coherently over time**.

In the context-navigation architecture, boundary conditions correspond to the
structural conditions that determine:

- which processing modes are viable
- which observables become relevant
- and which latent degrees of freedom can become active.

---

# 1 ARW Definition

In ARW, boundary conditions shape how degrees of freedom interact.

They do not only restrict trajectories or configurations.
Instead they act as **structural filters** that determine which interactions
can resonate and accumulate.

Boundary conditions may include:

- geometric constraints
- temporal constraints
- configurational restrictions
- coupling structures
- environmental conditions

They determine which degrees of freedom can remain latent and which become active.

---

# 2 Boundary Depth

Boundary conditions can operate at different depths:

Shallow boundaries

- local
- temporary
- weakly constraining

Deep boundaries

- global
- persistent
- structurally constraining

The interaction of boundaries of different depth is a major source of emergent behavior.

---

# 3 Relationship to Degrees of Freedom

Boundary conditions shape which degrees of freedom can interact coherently.

They determine:

- which freedoms remain latent
- which freedoms become dynamically active
- which combinations of freedoms are possible

Thus boundary conditions indirectly shape the **effective dimensionality**
of the system.

---

# 4 Translation to Cognitive Architecture

In the context-navigation architecture, boundary conditions correspond to
the structural conditions of the agent and its environment.

Examples include:

Environment structure
→ tasks, rules, physical environment

Agent architecture
→ network topology, memory structure, sensors

Interaction protocols
→ feedback signals, reward functions

These constraints determine which observables and interactions can accumulate
and influence the agent's behavior.

---

# 5 Boundary Conditions and Mode Viability

Boundary conditions determine which processing modes are viable.

A mode may only remain admissible if the boundary conditions allow the
assumptions of that mode to remain valid.

For example:

A heuristic mode may work under simple environmental constraints.

If the environment becomes more complex, the same mode may become inadmissible
because previously latent degrees of freedom become active.

---

# 6 Relationship to Latent Degrees of Freedom

Boundary conditions influence latent degrees of freedom by controlling
whether suppressed variables can accumulate influence.

Weak boundaries

→ allow many latent freedoms to interact

Strong structural boundaries

→ restrict which interactions can accumulate

Thus boundary conditions help determine when scope mismatch occurs.

---

# 7 Structural Design Perspective

One of the key implications of the ARW framework is that stability can often
be improved not by stronger control but by **better boundary design**.

Instead of suppressing dynamics, well-designed boundaries restrict
incompatible combinations of degrees of freedom.

This can reduce instability while preserving useful dynamical richness.

---

# 8 Relationship to Context Navigation

In a context-navigating agent, boundary conditions shape the landscape
in which modes compete.

They determine:

- which contexts appear frequently
- which modes remain viable
- how quickly admissibility can change

Thus boundary conditions indirectly structure the **mode ecology** of the agent.

---

# 9 Conceptual Summary

Boundary conditions act as resonance filters that determine which
interactions between degrees of freedom can accumulate over time.

In the cognitive architecture:

Boundary conditions correspond to the structural constraints imposed by
the environment and the agent's architecture.

They influence:

- which variables become relevant
- which modes remain admissible
- how latent degrees of freedom activate
- and how context navigation unfolds.
