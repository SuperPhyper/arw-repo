# Conflict Navigation as Nested Scope / Observable Calibration

**One-line claim:** Conflict navigation does not require a second method beside
calibration; it requires a *second calibration loop above the first* — the lower
loop calibrates observables within a fixed scope, the upper loop calibrates the
scope itself, and the two run on separated timescales.

This note is exploratory. It generalizes from a single, well-conditioned applied
case (a self-aware control loop / threshold calibration) toward a harder case
class (conflict) that has not yet been run. Per ARW methodology, a clean result in
one case class licenses nothing in another without re-derivation; the conflict
case below is therefore stated as structure-plus-open-questions, not as a result.

---

## 1. Origin

In the workshop series, the well-received pattern was an oscillation between
*dynamic problem identification* inside group sessions and *reflective
problem reworking* between sessions. The sessions had clear goals and did not
circle, because the solution was deliberately not foregrounded.

Crucially, that case was a **calibration**, not a conflict resolution: the goals
sat on the leadership / long-term-feedback level, and the in-session task was a
**threshold adjustment** — not "what is the solution" but "where do we set the
thresholds and how does everyone arrange themselves with that." The group
understood itself as a control loop, with a shared setpoint already in place.

This note isolates *why* that worked and asks what changes when the task is a
genuine conflict instead.

---

## 2. ARW-level structure (domain-neutral)

### 2.1 Two distinct task classes

| | Calibration | Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| Where the difference lives | *inside* the possibility space | the possibility space *itself* is contested |
| Form of the difference | a value on a shared scale | divergent observables / boundary constraints |
| Shared object | a setpoint exists ex ante | no shared setpoint; it must be constructed |
| Movement that resolves it | parameter adjustment | scope reconstruction |

In calibration, parties hold different *values* of a shared observable; the scope
is uncontested and the work is parameter convergence. In conflict, parties hold
*different observables* — partly incommensurable scopes — so there is no shared
question to converge on. This is structurally adjacent to an **F0 situation**: the
relevant observable for one party lies outside the shared scope's admissible
projections R(π) (see `docs/glossary/observable_range.md`), so the live process
does not converge, it polarizes.

### 2.2 The nested-loop construction

Conflict is then handled not by a new mechanism but by a second calibration loop
stacked above the first:

- **Lower loop — observable calibration.** Within a fixed scope, calibrate
  observable values. Shared setpoint assumed. Fast timescale (in-session).
- **Upper loop — scope calibration.** Calibrate *which observables / boundary
  constraints the shared scope admits*. No setpoint assumed ex ante. Slow
  timescale (between-session).

Two nested control loops, separated setpoints, separated clock rates.

### 2.3 Timescale separation is structural, not incidental

The scope adjustment **belongs** between sessions, because it *is* the reflective
reworking that already happens there. The live session is the wrong venue for it:
when the frame itself is openly contested, the live session polarizes rather than
converges. Holding the scope negotiation out of the live room is therefore the
structural answer to the polarization risk — the conflict is recognized as a scope
difference and lifted onto the slower loop, where it is workable without the
parties colliding in real time.

## 3. ART-level instantiation: mediation (Resonanz-Dialektik)

> *Level note: §2 is ARW (domain-neutral). §3 is an ART / Resonanz-Dialektik
> instantiation. The mappings below are interpretive and case-specific.*

### 3.1 Mediation presupposes a question in dispute

A mediation requires a **question in dispute**. The upper loop's goal is not to
*solve* the conflict but to **construct that question** — to find a scope in which
the parties' divergence appears as *one* negotiable question instead of two
mutually-bypassing concerns. Once the question in dispute stands, it is
calibratable, and the lower loop can do its work.

The construction of the shared question is **not formally derivable**. Which
question two parties recognize as *theirs in common* cannot be deduced; it must be
found such that each party recognizes itself in it. That finding is resonance work
(ART / Resonanz-Dialektik). ARW can verify *after the fact* whether the found scope
holds — does it shrink the F0 region, does it make more jointly describable — but
the finding itself sits on the ART side.

This gives the weighting hypothesis: **lower loop ARW-front, upper loop ART-front.**
The level separation (Regime-Wissenschaft vs. Regime-Theorie / Resonanz-Dialektik)
is precisely what lets the upper loop have its *own* method rather than forcing the
lower loop's parameter logic upward.

### 3.2 Success criterion (two-part)

A successful mediation:

1. produces a **negotiable scope**, *and*
2. **holds the group together.**

The second part is the sharper one; the first alone is too cheap. A negotiable
scope is trivial to produce if the group is allowed to shrink — drop the parties
that do not fit and the scope becomes coherent because the incommensurables are
gone. Part (2) forbids exactly that. The scope must be negotiable *and* carry
everyone. This is the real tension: negotiability pushes toward a narrow, sharp
scope; cohesion demands a wide one that excludes no party structurally. A good
mediation finds the scope that satisfies both at once.

### 3.3 Failure mode: structural fall-out (F0-analogue)

The precise failure condition is **not** "someone is dissatisfied." Dissatisfaction
means standing on the disliked side of a threshold *within* the scope — negotiable,
i.e. calibration. **Structural fall-out** means a party's observable no longer
appears in the scope at all — not outvoted, but invisible. This is the F0 situation
resolved one-sidedly: a scope that works for some because it no longer represents
the others. From inside it looks like success (the group converges), yet it is the
failure, because the convergence is bought at the cost of the unrepresented.

Consequence for the success measure: not "did everyone agree" but "is everyone
still represented in the scope." One can agree and still have fallen out — by
agreeing to the question that no longer contains one's own.

### 3.4 The operative criterion: participation incentive

Sharpening of §3.3, and the more useful form because it binds to something each
party can *feel* rather than something only the mediator can judge from outside:

> Every party needs a **stable regime that is calibratable**, otherwise there is no
> incentive to attend the mediation.

The incentive vanishes if either condition fails:

- **No stable regime** → no standing to negotiate from; the party is ground down
  *in* the process, every session reopens everything, nothing to calibrate, only to
  survive.
- **Stable but not calibratable** → frozen; participation changes nothing, so one
  may as well stay away.

Incentive exists only in the narrow band between: *secure enough not to be lost in
the process, mobile enough that participation can improve one's standing.* This is
close to a definition of negotiability as such — one negotiates only where one has
neither everything nor nothing to lose.

This tightens what the upper loop must deliver: not *a* negotiable scope, but one in
which **every** party retains such a calibratable regime. Not globally negotiable —
**locally negotiable for each**. That is a markedly stronger condition, and it is why
mediation is hard: one is not seeking the scope that satisfies the most parties, but
the scope under which no party loses its calibratable regime.

---

## 4. Indicator (open)

The participation criterion supplies an early-warning indicator that is a
*participation* measure, not an *output* measure: as long as every party still has
a reason to stay, the scope holds. The leading sign of impending structural
fall-out is therefore not dissatisfaction but **eroding incentive** — the moment a
party begins to question attendance itself.

**Open question:** Can incentive erosion be observed *before* a party actually
exits — i.e. is there a detectable precursor of fall-out while it is still
repairable, or is this currently only the trained facilitator's eye? (Candidate for
`docs/notes/open_questions.md`.)

---

## 5. Relation to existing structure

- The oscillation pattern (in-session dynamic / between-session reflective) maps
  onto the two loops as a temporal rhythm: live work on the fast lower loop, scope
  reworking on the slow upper loop. The oscillation that is virtuous in pure
  calibration can *invert* in conflict — a live session with a contested frame
  amplifies rather than corrects — which is the operational reason the scope
  negotiation is held to the between-session loop (§2.3).
- "Stable regime, calibratable" reuses the regime-stability notion from
  `docs/core/` at the level of a *party within a group scope*, rather than a state
  within a system scope. The transfer of the concept across these levels is itself
  unverified and should not be treated as established.

## 6. Status and next step

`note`. To advance toward `hypothesis`, the participation-incentive criterion
(§3.4) needs to be stated falsifiably against an actual conflict session — the
calibration case (2026-06-11) does **not** test it, since it had a shared setpoint
and no contested scope. The natural next step is a session deliberately posed as a
scope conflict (no shared setpoint, stable opposing camps) to see where the
two-loop structure begins to carry or tear.
