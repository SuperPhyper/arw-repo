# Crystallize the friction
### Surfacing the real oppositions before you converge

*A working step for contested frames. Slots in where the guide says "build the shared
question" but doesn't yet say how. Use it between sessions, on the slow loop.*

---

## The move the guide skips

When the read says Framing, the guide tells you to build a shared question — but it
treats the disagreement as something to get *past*. That's backwards. The opposed
positions aren't the obstacle in front of the compromise; they're the material the
compromise is built from. Friction you smooth over comes back later as a party quietly
falling out. Friction you **name sharply** becomes something you can actually work.

So before you converge, do the opposite of what feels natural: don't cluster, **collide.**

---

## Step 1 — Each party's own question, in its own words

Before any synthesis, get each party to state its *own* goal and the thing it's actually
tracking — in its own language, not yours:

> "What are you really measuring? What would have to be true for this to count as a win
> *for you*?"

No merging yet. You want the genuine, divergent versions on the table, including the
unglamorous ones.

## Step 2 — Pair for collision, not for theme

A gallery walk clusters by similarity — and clustering *erases* friction, because it pulls
like things together and smooths the opposites away. Here you want the reverse. Lay the
native questions side by side and find where they **pull against each other**:

- Not "these three are about cost"
- But "A's supplier-feasibility and C's carbon threshold pull the *same dial* in opposite
  directions."

The output of this step is a list of **collisions**, not a list of themes.

## Step 3 — Name each collision so both sides own it

Give every friction point a wording that *both* parties recognize as their shared tension
— the conflict version of "each party finds itself in it." This is the crystallization:
diffuse "we don't agree" becomes a sharp, named opposition with edges.

This matters because **a diffuse friction can't be corrected; a named one can.** Until
the opposition has a name both sides accept, there's nothing concrete to work on — only
a mood. Naming turns the friction into a place you can stand and push.

---

## Step 4 — Diagnose each named friction

Not all friction is the same kind, and the kind decides what correction is even possible.
Walk each named collision through three questions, in order:

| Ask | If… | It's | What it means | The move |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Is the party's real concern even *in* the question? | No | **Missing piece** | A workable answer may exist — you just can't see it, because one side isn't represented | Put the missing concern on the table, then re-pair (back to Step 2) |
| It's in — could one setting satisfy both *in principle*, but only one side can move the dial? | Yes | **Uneven leverage** | The opposition is about *power*, not content — who is allowed to move what | Rebalance reach: sequencing, reciprocal moves, shared authority over the parameter |
| Is there *no* setting that works for both, under any framing you can reach? | Yes | **No overlap** | The opposition is about values or identity, not numbers | Don't force a compromise — reshape into a different question if you can, otherwise name it as structural |

The order matters: a **Missing piece** misread as **No overlap** kills a solvable
process; a **No overlap** treated as a **Missing piece** grinds the room down chasing a
compromise that isn't there.

## Step 5 — Work the compromise *at* the friction point

Only now, with the opposition named and typed, is an acceptable compromise constructible
— and it's usually local, not global: at *this* collision, find the threshold, the phased
path, or the rebalanced reach in which both sides still recognize their position. The
compromise isn't negotiated in the fog; it's worked out of a friction point that's
standing sharp and still.

---

## The second output: knowing what *can't* be solved

Some crystallized frictions are **No overlap** — and that is not a failure of the tool,
it's its second most useful result. Crystallization makes the un-negotiable opposition
*honestly visible*, instead of hiding it under a forced convergence that sheds a party
later. Knowing which friction is structural — and saying so — is itself a finding worth
the session.

---

## One caution on timing

Crystallizing a sharp opposition **in a live room can polarize** instead of clarify — the
same risk as working a contested frame live. Keep the friction work mostly on the slow,
between-session loop. Live, the most you usually want is to *name* a friction point and
park it ("there's a real opposition here between X and Y — that's worth its own work");
the full surfacing, diagnosis, and compromise belong off the live clock, brought back as
a clarified set of named frictions.
