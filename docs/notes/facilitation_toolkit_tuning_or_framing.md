# Tuning or Framing?
### A field guide and toolkit for multi-stakeholder sessions

*Distilled from the low-carbon concrete standard-update process. Merged draft: the
diagnostic spine and the operational toolkit in one document.*

---

## Start here: two questions, not one

Before you design an agenda for a stuck or tense group, you're really asking **two
separate questions** — and most facilitation goes wrong by collapsing them into one.

1. **Can the group calibrate together?** — Do they share a picture of the problem,
   is a workable shared outcome visible, and is it reachable in steps they can manage?
2. **Will the group move toward it?** — Even when a good shared outcome is in plain
   view and everyone would prefer it, will they actually walk there?

Different questions, different remedies. **Question 1 you can work on directly** — the
whole toolkit in Part I is for it. **Question 2 you mostly can't manufacture** — but you
can make the move cheaper, and you can be honest when the will isn't there. Keep them
apart. Collapsing them is the single most common design error: running a process as if
better facilitation could supply a willingness that no process supplies.

This document is organized around that split. **Part I** is everything you do to help a
group *be able* to calibrate. **Part II** is the harder, narrower question of whether they
*will*. **Part III** is the reference apparatus — checklists, templates, documentation
patterns — that supports the work in Part I.

---

# PART I — Can the group calibrate together?

Everything here serves one end: making a workable shared outcome **exist, become
visible, and be reachable in steps the group can manage.** That is the work you control.

## 1. The core read: Tuning or Framing?

- **Tuning** — everyone agrees what "done" looks like and what's in and out of bounds.
  The work is adjusting settings until they fit. Disagreement is about *where* to set
  the dial, not *whether* it's the right dial. → **Facilitate toward convergence.**
- **Framing** — the frame itself is contested. Parties don't share a definition of
  success, or they aren't measuring the same things. → **Don't tune. Build the shared
  frame first.**

> Most failure in technical and policy processes comes from running a **Framing** fight
> as if it were a **Tuning** exercise. The room talks past itself, and more facilitation
> only sharpens the collision.

### The 60-second read

Five yes/no questions. Mostly **yes** → Tuning. Any clear **no** → treat as Framing
until resolved.

1. **Shared bounds** — does everyone roughly agree what's in scope and what's off the table?
2. **Inherited definition of success** — did "what good looks like" exist *before* this
   group convened (a mandate, a regulation, a prior commitment)? Or must they invent it?
3. **Shared measures** — is everyone looking at the same numbers and criteria, and do
   they trust them as comparable?
4. **Everyone represented** — is every party's real hard constraint visible — including
   the unglamorous ones (a supplier's capacity limit, a cost floor, a political non-starter)?
5. **Everyone has room to move** — can each party give a little without losing everything?

Questions 1–3 sort Tuning from Framing. Questions **4 and 5** are the ones that quietly
turn a working Tuning process into a blow-up if you miss them.

### What the read tells you to do

- **If Tuning:** keep the live room on parameters only (thresholds, criteria, timelines).
  Do the frame and definition work *between* sessions, never live in the group.
- **If Framing:** stop tuning. Name the contested frame — where definitions of success
  diverge, what each party is actually tracking. Then either build a shared-enough frame
  (which converts it to Tuning) or be honest that it isn't ready to converge.

## 2. The two loops, two clocks

Group work runs on two nested loops on **separated clock rates.** The discipline is not
*where* you do each — it is that you never run both at once in the same room.

- **Inner loop — live calibration (fast, in-room).** Adjust preferences, interpretations,
  thresholds within a *stable, shared frame*. This is where momentum and visible progress
  come from. It works only when the frame is agreed. If the frame itself is contested, the
  live room destabilizes or polarizes.
- **Outer loop — reflective reframing (slow, between sessions).** Reconstruct the frame
  itself, resolve differences in how people see the problem, assimilate new information,
  build a shared question. This needs slow thinking, synthesis, and tolerance for
  incompatible interpretations — none of which a live room provides.

**Why they must stay separated.** When the group disagrees about the frame, trying to
fix it in real time produces confusion or polarization. The oscillation that is virtuous
in a pure Tuning process *inverts* under Framing: a live session with a contested frame
amplifies the difference instead of correcting it. So you lift frame work onto the slow
loop, where the parties can work it without colliding in real time.

Protect the inner loop by: identifying frame differences early; refusing to force
convergence on an unstable frame; moving frame reconstruction into the outer loop; and
entering each new session with a clarified or revised frame.

*The outer-loop passes are laid out in the companion note
[Between the sessions](facilitation_between_the_sessions.md).*

## 3. Preparing for a session or a multi-session process

Good preparation is kinematic work: it makes the shared picture exist *before* anyone is
in the room under pressure. Two dimensions, both needed — preparing the **participants**
and preparing the **frame** they'll work inside.

### 3.1 Prework packets

Prework packets let participants arrive oriented, informed, and ready to engage — and
they especially serve people who do their best thinking by reflecting before they speak.

Timing:
- For a meeting later in the week, send the packet **at the start of the week** (e.g.
  Monday for a Thursday session).
- Avoid sending important material at the end of the week, where it gets lost.

A packet typically includes: the agenda; the purpose of the meeting; key questions and
decision points; light reflection prompts; optional background; a link to a pre-meeting
survey; and a short "what to review before we meet." Keep reflection prompts **light**
unless participants got background materials well in advance.

### 3.2 Surveys, used on purpose

Surveys support multiple processing styles and surface alignment, concerns, and confusion
without forcing anyone to think on the spot. Use them at distinct stages:

- **Before** — capture early perspectives, surface questions and concerns, let people
  think without pressure.
- **During** — quick temperature checks, show the spread of viewpoints, give quieter
  participants a voice.
- **Between** — explore complex issues, gather deeper reflection, refine earlier input,
  prepare frame or scope work. *This is where outer-loop reframing largely happens.*
- **After** — feedback on clarity, pacing, and design to inform the next session.

Surveys may be anonymous and can carry optional demographic or contact fields.

### 3.3 Alternating session types (multi-session processes)

Alternate two kinds of session, and don't do both in one meeting:

- **Stability sessions** — review standards, parameters, constraints; provide updates;
  calibrate within a known frame. Emphasis: clarity, grounding, predictable structure.
- **Exploration sessions** — consider alternatives, reframe, bring in new information,
  widen the possibility space. Emphasis: openness, curiosity.

Alternating keeps coherence without freezing the process.

## 4. Running the live session

The in-room machinery for the inner loop. The aim is to keep the room coherent, move
through phases productively, and keep participation broad — so that *whatever* shared
picture exists actually gets built rather than dominated.

### 4.1 Opening reflection

Orients people, lowers pressure, moves the group from private mental models into shared
conversation. A short poll or quick written response works well. Examples: "What's one
thing you hope we accomplish today?" / "Which topic matters most to you right now?" /
"What question is top of mind as we begin?"

### 4.2 Multi-scale participation

Move through three stages to keep dominant voices from setting the early frame:
**individual reflection** (quiet time to form a first thought) → **pair or small-group
discussion** (compare and refine) → **whole-group synthesis** (themes emerge across
perspectives). This reduces pressure and the over-reliance on fast verbal processing.

### 4.3 Tools for mixed processing styles

Offer several entry points so the room isn't built only for the quickest talkers: silent
reflection and note-taking; quick polls; small-group breakouts; visual boards; chat
submissions; structured turn-taking.

### 4.4 The four-lens check

Keep discussion balanced by drawing on four perspectives — a healthy discussion touches
each at least once:

- **Patterns & meaning** — what structures or trends matter?
- **Experience & examples** — what's been seen or tried before?
- **Possibilities & options** — what alternatives or new ideas exist?
- **Practical realities** — what would this look like in practice?

When the conversation goes narrow or stuck, introduce a question from a lens not yet
represented.

### 4.5 Comment gallery walk

Collect written input (sticky notes, chat, forms, boards), cluster the responses, and
walk the group through the themes visually. Inclusive, low-pressure, and surfaces
complexity without requiring everyone to speak aloud.

### 4.6 Pacing and mode shifts

A room moves through modes: **steady** (grounded, coherent), **fast** (reactive,
pressured, convergent), **protective** (tense, rigid, withdrawing). If it tips into a
protective mode: pause; name neutrally what you're noticing; give a short reflection or
breathing break; reframe the question or return to a known reference point. This restores
stability without forcing it.

### 4.7 Closing check-out

A brief closing question lets you read clarity and readiness: "What feels clear now?" /
"What question remains?" / "What stood out as most useful?"

## 5. When it's Framing: build the shared frame between sessions

If the read says Framing, the live room is the wrong venue. The work belongs on the slow
loop, and it has a single goal: construct a **shared question** that every party
recognizes as *their own* — one negotiable question in place of two mutually bypassing
concerns. Once that question stands, it's calibratable, and the live room can do its job.

### 5.1 Identifying frame differences

Signs you're in a frame conflict, not a parameter disagreement: people consistently
talking past one another; incompatible definitions of the problem; diverging readings of
what the decision is even about; disagreement over what counts as evidence or progress.
Trying to resolve these live produces stagnation or escalation.

### 5.2 Reframing on the outer loop

Reframing needs slow thinking. Use surveys, reflection prompts, analysis, facilitator
synthesis, and written materials that compare or integrate perspectives. The output is a
shared question, brought back into the next session as a clarified frame. The concrete
surfacing-and-collision procedure is in the companion note
[Crystallize the friction](facilitation_crystallize_the_friction.md).

### 5.3 Maintaining representation — the real success test

A shared frame is *not* successful merely because the group converges. It's successful
when **every party still appears in it.** A negotiable frame is cheap to produce if the
group is allowed to shrink — drop the parties that don't fit and the frame goes coherent
because the incommensurables are gone. That is the failure, dressed as success: a frame
that works for some *because it no longer represents the others.*

So the test is not "did everyone agree" but "is everyone still represented." A party can
agree and still have fallen out — by agreeing to a question that no longer contains its
own concern. The frame must be **negotiable enough to be sharp and wide enough to carry
everyone at once.** That tension is the actual difficulty of the work.

### 5.4 Early-warning sign: a constraint going invisible

The thing that wrecks an otherwise-working process is a real constraint going **quiet**:
a supplier's hard limit stops getting mentioned, a political non-starter gets assumed
away, a safety or performance constraint gets treated as flexible. When a party's hard
limit drops out of the shared picture, that party loses its reason to stay — and
disengages, or blows it up later.

**Recovery move:** the moment a constraint goes quiet, put it back on the table
explicitly, even if it slows things down. Keeping every party's hard limit *visible and
still adjustable* is what keeps every party at the table.

---

## 6. The constructive move: build the goal *with* the group

The strongest way to turn a Framing fight into a Tuning process is to **build the shared
goal together, not hand it down.** A goal the group builds is one where each party has
quietly placed its own acceptable outcome inside the target — so everyone has somewhere
to stand. A goal imposed from outside often leaves someone with no acceptable position,
and that person checks out.

This is powerful, and it has a hard limit — the limit that is all of Part II: building a
shared goal makes a good shared outcome *exist and become visible.* It does **not** make
the group *walk there.* Hold that line. Constructing the landscape does not change whether
they descend it.

---

## 7. Participation incentive — the operative criterion

This is the criterion that binds Part I together, and the reason to do all of the above.
A party stays at the table only when it has **a stable position that is also negotiable.**
The incentive vanishes if either half fails:

- **No stable position** → nothing to negotiate *from*; the party is ground down in the
  process, every session reopens everything, and there's nothing to calibrate, only to
  survive.
- **Stable but not negotiable** → frozen; participation changes nothing, so one may as
  well stay away.

Incentive lives only in the narrow band between: *secure enough not to be lost in the
process, mobile enough that showing up can improve one's standing.* This is close to a
definition of negotiability itself — one negotiates only where one has neither everything
nor nothing to lose.

The strong form: the frame you build must leave **every** party with such a position —
not globally negotiable, but **locally negotiable for each.** You are not looking for the
frame that satisfies the most parties; you are looking for the frame under which no party
loses its foothold. That is why this work is hard.

This also gives you the leading indicator. The first sign of impending fall-out is not
dissatisfaction — dissatisfaction means standing on the disliked side of a threshold
you can still move, which is healthy negotiation. The real precursor is **eroding
incentive**: the moment a party begins to question attendance itself. Watch for it
directly — people questioning the value of coming, difficulty articulating where their
concern fits, persistent non-alignment with the frame.

# PART II — Will the group move toward it?

Part I makes a good outcome *exist, visible, and reachable.* None of it makes a group
*go.* This part is the half you do not control — and the honesty about that limit is the
most useful thing this guide carries.

## 8. It's not just the destination — it's the path

Two groups can agree on the exact same goal, and one succeeds while the other falls apart.
The difference is the **path** — the intermediate steps between here and there.

Agreement on the destination is not enough. A shared *target* is not a reachable *target*.
Check the **hardest step in the middle**:

- Is there an intermediate stage where someone has to give up a lot before getting
  anything back?
- Is the biggest single move larger than what this group can actually manage at once?

If the hardest intermediate step is bigger than the group's real capacity to move, the
process stalls *even though everyone agrees on the goal.* So the operative question isn't
"do they agree on the destination?" — it's **"is the hardest step on the path within what
this group can actually do in one move?"**

**This is your one real lever between precondition and outcome:** break the path into
smaller steps. You can't create willingness, but you can shrink how much willingness each
step demands. Everything you control in Part II runs through this.

---

## 9. The wall you can't push through

Sometimes the group shares the picture completely — same frame, same measures, everyone
can see the good shared outcome, everyone would even *prefer* it — and they still don't
move toward it.

This is **not** a Framing problem, and no amount of reframing fixes it. It's a willingness
problem: each party is better off, or feels safer, not being the one to move first. Signs:

- Everyone privately agrees on the better outcome, but no one commits.
- "We'll move when they move" — from both sides.
- A stable, mutually-disliked status quo that nobody will be first to leave.

**Not in your control.** You cannot manufacture a genuine willingness to get somewhere
together. A process does not create it; the right procedure does not produce it. The
cooperative disposition — tracking a shared goal rather than each party's own best move —
is a *precondition* you inherit, not a product you can engineer. If it isn't there at all,
that is real information. **Name it honestly, rather than running more sessions that look
productive and change nothing.** This is the claim most facilitation quietly denies, and
the one most worth holding.

**In your control:**

- Lower the cost of the *first* move — smaller steps, reciprocal or simultaneous moves,
  reversible trials.
- Make moves visible and verifiable, so "we'll move when they move" can actually be
  acted on.
- Keep every party's hard constraint on the table, so no one's reason to stay disappears.

The honest version: a shared goal plus a low-barrier path lets a *little* willingness go a
long way. But some willingness has to be there. The procedure can lower how much will each
step requires; it cannot raise the will. That is the line between what you can do and what
you can only name.

# PART III — Reference toolkit

The operational apparatus for Part I. Subordinate to the diagnostic, not a substitute for
it: these tools run a session well *once you know whether you're tuning or framing.*

## 10. Facilitator checklists

**Before each session**
- Send the prework packet.
- Prepare the pre-meeting survey.
- Draft an agenda with clear purpose statements.
- Prepare visual supports (boards, slides, polls) and spaces for written input.
- Review pre-meeting survey results.
- **Run the 60-second read; mark each item as inner-loop (tune live) or outer-loop
  (frame between sessions).**
- Prepare questions that support balanced participation.

**During each session**
- Open with a light reflection.
- Clarify the purpose.
- Use multi-scale participation (individual → small group → whole group).
- Introduce a missing lens when the conversation narrows.
- Use a gallery walk for written input.
- Mark transitions clearly; keep the two clocks from mixing.
- Pause and reset if the room goes protective.
- Close with a check-out.

**After each session**

*Internal (not shared):* review notes and patterns; identify where frame work is needed;
integrate survey results; draft reflections; prepare the outer-loop reframing.

*External (shared):* send a concise recap; list themes and open questions; clarify
decisions and next steps; share upcoming dates; include an optional feedback survey.

## 11. Templates

**Prework packet** — Purpose · Agenda · Key questions · Background materials · Light
reflection prompts · Survey link · What to review.

**Agenda** — Welcome & purpose · Opening reflection · Topic 1 (individual → small group →
whole group) · Topic 2 (discussion or polls) · Synthesis and next steps · Closing
reflection.

**Survey question types** — light reflection · preference checks · scenario reactions ·
open-ended.

**Meeting summary** — Purpose · Themes heard · Clarifications · Open questions · Next
steps · Upcoming dates.

**Multi-session arc** — Session 1: orientation and early perspectives · Session 2:
stability-focused synthesis · Session 3: exploration and learning · Session 4: refinement
and tuning · Session 5: decision-focused synthesis · Final: documentation and
communication.

## 12. Documentation and decision transparency

- **Internal working notes** — raw survey data, unfiltered comments, patterns and
  contradictions, interpretive analysis, early scenario outlines. For understanding the
  system; not shared directly.
- **External recaps** — short, theme-based, free of technical detail, oriented toward next
  steps. Structure: purpose · themes · clarifications · decisions · open questions · next
  steps and dates.
- **Across the process** — compile major themes, document how decisions evolved, summarize
  rationales, integrate the survey evidence, present final results clearly.

## 13. Building an inclusive meeting culture

Offer multiple participation modes; be transparent about process and decisions; normalize
clarification questions; provide pauses and reflection time; acknowledge different working
styles; avoid overload; keep meetings predictable and clearly signposted. When people
understand the process and can engage in the way they think best, the room gets steadier
and the work gets done.

---

## What this guide can and can't tell you

- It **can** tell you whether a group *can* calibrate together — whether a workable shared
  outcome exists, is visible, and is reachable in steps they can manage. That's Part I,
  and it's the part you control.
- It **cannot** tell you whether they *will* — that depends on a willingness to get
  somewhere together, which no process creates. That's Part II, and the most useful thing
  you can do with it is read it honestly.
- The one thing connecting the two: **keep the path's steps small,** so whatever
  willingness is present is enough to clear them.
