# Tuning or Framing?
### A field guide for multi-stakeholder sessions — v2

*Distilled from the low-carbon concrete standard-update process. Revised.*

---

## Start here: two questions, not one

Before you design an agenda for a stuck or tense group, you're really asking **two
separate questions** — and most facilitation goes wrong by collapsing them into one.

1. **Does the group share a picture of the problem?** — can they even see the same thing?
2. **If they share it, are they willing to move toward a shared outcome?** — will they walk to it?

Different answers, different remedies. The first you can work on directly. The second
you mostly can't manufacture — but you *can* make it easier to act on. Keep them apart.

---

## Question 1 — Tuning or Framing?

- **Tuning** — everyone agrees what "done" looks like and what's in and out of bounds.
  The work is adjusting settings until they fit. Disagreement is about *where* to set
  the dial, not *whether* it's the right dial. → **Facilitate toward convergence.**
- **Framing** — the frame itself is contested. Parties don't share a definition of
  success, or they aren't measuring the same things. → **Don't tune. Build the shared
  frame first.**

> Most failure in technical/policy processes comes from running a **Framing** fight
> as if it were a **Tuning** exercise.

### The 60-second read

Five yes/no questions. Mostly **yes** → Tuning. Any clear **no** → treat as Framing
until resolved.

1. **Shared bounds** — does everyone roughly agree what's in scope and what's off the table?
2. **Inherited definition of success** — did "what good looks like" exist *before* this
   group convened (a mandate, a regulation, a prior commitment)? Or must they invent it?
3. **Shared measures** — is everyone looking at the same numbers/criteria, and do they
   trust them as comparable?
4. **Everyone represented** — is every party's real hard constraint visible — including
   the unglamorous ones (a supplier's capacity limit, a cost floor, a political non-starter)?
5. **Everyone has room to move** — can each party give a little without losing everything?

Questions 1–3 sort Tuning from Framing. Questions **4 and 5** are the ones that quietly
turn a working Tuning process into a blow-up if you miss them.

### What to do
- **If Tuning:** keep the live room on parameters only (thresholds, criteria, timelines).
  Do the frame/definition work *between* sessions, never live in the group. Run two
  clocks — a fast in-room loop and a slow between-session loop — and don't let them mix.
- **If Framing:** stop tuning. Name the contested frame (where definitions of success
  diverge, what each party is actually tracking). Then either build a shared-enough frame
  (which converts it to Tuning) or be honest that it isn't ready to converge.

---

## The constructive move: build the goal *with* the group

The strongest way to turn a Framing fight into a Tuning process is to **build the shared
goal together, not hand it down.** A goal the group builds is one where each party has
quietly placed its own acceptable outcome inside the target — so everyone has somewhere
to stand. A goal imposed from outside often leaves someone with no acceptable position,
and that person checks out.

This is powerful, but it has a hard limit, and the limit is Question 2: building a shared
goal makes a good shared outcome *exist and become visible* — it does **not** make the
group *walk there*. Hold that line.

---

## It's not just the destination — it's the path

Two groups can agree on the exact same goal, and one succeeds while the other falls apart.
The difference is the **path** — the intermediate steps between here and the goal.

Agreement on the destination is not enough. Check the **hardest step in the middle**:

- Is there an intermediate stage where someone has to give up a lot before getting anything back?
- Is the biggest single move larger than what this group can actually manage at once?

If the hardest intermediate step is bigger than the group's real capacity to move, the
process stalls *even though everyone agrees on the goal*. So the operative question isn't
"do they agree on the destination?" — it's **"is the hardest step on the path within what
this group can actually do in one move?"**

**Your main lever here:** break the path into smaller steps. You can't create willingness,
but you can shrink how much willingness each step demands.

---

## Question 2 — the wall you can't push through

Sometimes the group shares the picture completely — same frame, same measures, everyone
can see the good shared outcome, everyone would even *prefer* it — and they still don't
move toward it.

This is **not** a Framing problem, and no amount of reframing fixes it. It's a willingness
problem: each party is better off (or feels safer) not being the one to move first. Signs:
- Everyone privately agrees on the better outcome, but no one commits.
- "We'll move when they move" — from both sides.
- A stable, mutually-disliked status quo that nobody will be first to leave.

**Not in your control:** you cannot manufacture a genuine willingness to get somewhere
together. A process does not create it. If it isn't there at all, that's real information —
name it honestly rather than running more sessions that look productive and change nothing.

**In your control:**
- Lower the cost of the *first* move (smaller steps, reciprocal or simultaneous moves,
  reversible trials).
- Make moves visible and verifiable, so "we'll move when they move" can actually happen.
- Keep every party's hard constraint on the table, so no one's reason to stay disappears.

The honest version: a shared goal plus a low-barrier path lets a *little* willingness go a
long way. But some willingness has to be there. That part is a precondition, not an output.

---

## Early-warning sign (watch mid-process)

The thing that wrecks an otherwise-working process is a real constraint going **invisible**:
a supplier's hard limit stops getting mentioned, a political non-starter gets assumed away,
a safety/performance constraint gets treated as flexible. When a party's hard limit drops
out of the shared picture, that party loses its reason to stay — and disengages, or blows
it up later.

**Recovery move:** the moment a constraint goes quiet, put it back on the table explicitly,
even if it slows things down. Keeping every party's hard limit *visible and still adjustable*
is what keeps every party at the table.

---

## Healthy rhythm (for a process that runs over time)

- **Alternate session types:** stability sessions (work the standard / the dials) and
  learning sessions (new info, outside input). Don't do both in one meeting.
- **In-room cadence:** present → let people process internally → then process out loud together.
- **Between sessions:** reflective re-working → staff synthesis → check against participant
  feedback → return with a proposal.

---

## What this guide can and can't tell you

- It **can** tell you whether a group *can* calibrate together — whether a workable shared
  outcome exists, is visible, and is reachable in steps they can manage.
- It **cannot** tell you whether they *will* — that depends on a willingness to get somewhere
  together, which no process creates.
- The one thing connecting the two: **keep the path's steps small**, so whatever willingness
  is present is enough to clear them.
