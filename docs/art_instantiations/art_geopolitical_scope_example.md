# ART Instantiation Example

## Geopolitical Conflict Scope: Persian Gulf System

### 1 Scope Definition

**Scope:**\
Regional geopolitical interaction system centered on the Persian Gulf
and the Strait of Hormuz.

**Primary actors** - Iran - United States - Israel

**Secondary actors** - Gulf states - regional proxy groups - global
energy markets - international political institutions

**System coupling variables** - maritime transport routes - energy flows
(oil, LNG) - military infrastructure - domestic political stability

The scope focuses on how **boundary conditions constrain admissible
strategic regimes** and how **system pressure reshapes the attractor
landscape** of possible conflict dynamics.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 2 Boundary Condition Analysis

In ART, actors do not primarily differ by "strength" but by **their
boundary-condition sets**, which determine which regimes are
structurally admissible.

## United States / Israel

Key boundary conditions include: - strong casualty aversion - political
accountability and public opinion - alliance coordination requirements -
global economic interdependence - logistical complexity of large-scale
operations

These constraints significantly restrict the set of admissible regimes.

**High-admissibility regimes** - precision air campaigns - limited
maritime operations - intelligence operations - economic pressure /
sanctions

**Low-admissibility regimes** - large-scale ground invasion - prolonged
territorial occupation - open-ended regional war

The system therefore operates in a **narrow but technologically powerful
action space**.

## Iran

Iran operates under a different boundary-condition regime: - long-term
sanctions environment - military asymmetry against US air power - strong
territorial familiarity - decentralized regional influence networks -
higher tolerance for prolonged attritional conflict

These conditions expand the range of **indirect or asymmetric regimes**
that remain admissible.

**High-admissibility regimes** - proxy warfare - maritime disruption -
infrastructure targeting - economic pressure via chokepoints

**Low-admissibility regimes** - direct conventional confrontation with
US forces - sustained open battlefield engagements

Iran therefore operates in a **wider but more resource-constrained
regime space**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 3 Regime Space

Given the above boundary conditions, the geopolitical system contains
several possible **strategic regimes**:

R1 --- Limited air conflict\
R2 --- Proxy escalation\
R3 --- Maritime disruption in Hormuz\
R4 --- Economic attrition conflict\
R5 --- Internal destabilization of actors\
R6 --- Regional war expansion

Not all regimes are equally admissible.

Boundary conditions strongly suppress certain transitions, particularly
those requiring sustained conventional warfare.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 4 Attractor Landscape

Within ART, geopolitical orders can be interpreted as **attractors in a
stability landscape**.

Each attractor is characterized by: - depth (resistance to transition) -
breadth (range of compatible states) - coupling strength to external
systems

## A1 --- Contained Conflict

Characteristics: - periodic military strikes - continued economic
pressure - limited escalation

This attractor is relatively broad and historically stable.

## A2 --- Regional Escalation

Characteristics: - proxy expansion - attacks on energy infrastructure -
economic ripple effects

This attractor is deeper under high system pressure but less stable long
term.

## A3 --- Internal Political Destabilization

Characteristics: - domestic unrest - political fragmentation - regime
stress

This attractor depends strongly on internal boundary conditions and
external pressure.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 5 Pressure-Induced Attractor Dynamics

External threat can modify the landscape itself.

In ART terms:

external pressure\
→ collapse of internal partitions\
→ increased regime cohesion\
→ attractor deepening

This mechanism can temporarily increase stability within a system under
siege conditions.

However, this **pressure-induced depth increase** often occurs alongside
a **reduction in attractor breadth**, producing systems that are:

-   short-term robust
-   long-term structurally fragile

This distinction is critical when interpreting conflict dynamics.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 6 Chokepoint Coupling

The Strait of Hormuz functions as a **high-leverage coupling interface**
between regional conflict and global systems.

Properties: - high throughput of global energy flows - limited
geographic redundancy - strong economic ripple effects

In ART terms, such chokepoints act as **landscape amplifiers**:

local disruption\
→ global system pressure increase\
→ reshaping of regime admissibility

This coupling can transform a regional confrontation into a global
systemic stress event.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 7 Interpretation

From an ART perspective, the current system dynamics may not indicate
the formation of a new stable geopolitical paradigm.

Instead they may reflect: - pressure-induced deepening of existing
attractors - temporary regime compression - reduced internal partition
diversity

Such configurations can appear highly stable during crisis periods while
remaining structurally fragile over longer timescales.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 8 Relation to Asymmetry Models

Game-theoretic descriptions of asymmetric conflict (such as Jiang's "law
of asymmetry") can be interpreted within ART as **emergent effects of
asymmetric boundary conditions**.

Rather than representing behavioral laws, these patterns arise because
different actors operate within **different admissible regime spaces**.
