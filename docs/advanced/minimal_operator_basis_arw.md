# Minimal Operator Basis for ARW

## Overview

This document proposes a **two-layer minimal operator basis** for the ARW framework.

The key idea is that not all mathematical operations required for concrete models
are equally fundamental at the structural level.

A distinction is made between:

1. **Structural primitive operators** required to describe system structure and scope relations.
2. **Quantitative operators** required once numerical or metric structure is introduced.

This separation allows ARW to remain structurally minimal while still supporting
fully quantitative instantiations in ART models.

---

# 1. Structural Primitive Operators (ARW-Core)

The following three operators appear to form a minimal structural basis.

| Operator | Structural role |
|---|---|
| Composition | chaining of dependencies and transformations |
| Product construction | extension of state space / coupling of degrees of freedom |
| Projection | restriction or selection of admissible states |

These operators allow the representation of:

- dependency chains
- coupling between subsystems
- restriction to admissible regions
- aggregation and coarse-graining
- state space extension
- time coupling as a derived construction

### Example

Time coupling can be expressed as:

state extension:  
X → X × T

followed by dependency:

ẋ = f(x, t)

This uses product construction and composition without requiring
additional primitive operators.

### The central minimality question

Can **projection** itself be derived from product and composition
in sufficiently rich spaces?

If X is a product space X = A × B, then the canonical projection

π_A: A × B → A

is definable purely in terms of the product structure — it is the
unique morphism making the product diagram commute.

This suggests that in spaces with sufficient product structure,
projection is not independent but **follows from product construction**.

If this holds generally, the structural basis reduces to two operators:

{composition, product construction}

ARW would then be structurally equivalent to a fragment of category theory:
objects (state spaces), morphisms (compositions), and products —
the minimal language for describing dependency and coupling structure.

Whether this two-operator basis is sufficient for all BC classes,
or whether projection must remain primitive in spaces without
full product structure, is the **core open question of the operator program**
and is addressed directly in Section 6.

---

# 2. Derived Dynamic Structures

Dynamic BC classes emerge when structural operators are coupled with time.

Examples:

| BC class | Operator chain |
|---|---|
| Restriction | projection |
| Coupling | product + composition |
| Aggregation | projection + composition |
| Symmetry breaking | projection ∘ parameter variation |
| Forcing | product(time) + composition |
| Dissipation | product(time) + composition leading to attractor projection |

Dynamic BCs therefore arise from **operator chains applied to extended state spaces**.

### Dissipation as asymptotic projection

The entry for Dissipation — `product(time) + composition leading to attractor projection`
— requires clarification of the phrase "leading to".

Dissipation does not *contain* a projection operator in its local composition chain.
The projection is not present at any finite time t.

Rather, Dissipation produces projection **as a limit**:

lim_{t→∞} φ_t(x) = A   for all x in basin(A)

where A is the attractor and φ_t is the flow.

Structurally: the flow φ_t, generated by `product(time) + composition`,
converges to a map whose image is A — which is precisely the image of
a projection onto A.

This answers Q-new-B directly:

> Dynamic BCs can generate static BC structure, but only as asymptotic
> limits of the operator chain under time evolution — not as finite compositions.

The static/dynamic distinction therefore corresponds to:

- **finite composition depth** → static BC (Restriction, Coupling, ...)
- **infinite composition depth (limit)** → dynamic BC generating static structure

This introduces a natural **derivational depth ordering** on BC classes,
with static BCs at finite depth and dynamic BCs at infinite depth.

---

# 3. Quantitative Operators (ART Layer)

Once numerical structure is introduced, additional operators become necessary.

| Operator | Role |
|---|---|
| Addition | superposition of contributions |
| Multiplication / scaling | proportional interaction and decay |

These operators allow explicit dynamical equations such as:

ẋ = -λx + A sin(ωt)

which combine scaling and additive forcing.

At this level, differential operators, integrals, and domain-specific
mathematical structures may also appear.

---

# 3a. Stochastic Extension

The quantitative operator layer requires extension when stochastic or
probabilistic BCs are introduced — as in population dynamics (CASE-0005)
and social systems.

Stochasticity introduces a new degree of freedom: the sample space Ω
of a probability space (Ω, F, P).

Structurally, this is analogous to time coupling:

**Stochastic extension** = product construction extending X by Ω:

X → X × Ω

followed by composition with a measurability condition (F-measurability
as a structural constraint on the dependency chain).

Under this reading, stochastic BCs are **probabilistic couplings** —
product constructions with a distinguished random degree of freedom,
analogous to time couplings with a distinguished temporal degree of freedom.

The expectation operator E[·] then appears as the structural analogue
of time integration: a composition that collapses the Ω dimension,
producing a deterministic quantity from a stochastic one.

This suggests the stochastic layer requires one additional operator:

| Operator | Role | Layer |
|---|---|---|
| Expectation / integration over Ω | collapse of stochastic dimension | ART stochastic sublayer |

**Is Expectation a form of Projection?**

Expectation projects from the space of random variables L²(Ω) onto
the subspace of constants — it is the orthogonal projection onto
the trivial sub-σ-algebra.

Under this reading, Expectation is not a new primitive but a
**metric-weighted projection** on the stochastic extension space.
It requires the ART quantitative layer (multiplication for weighting,
addition for integration) but no new structural primitive.

The stochastic basis therefore does not expand {composition, product, projection}
at the structural level — it instantiates the same three operators
on a richer space that includes Ω as an additional factor.

---

# 4. Layered Operator Hierarchy

The resulting hierarchy is:

Structural operators (ARW core):

composition  
product construction  
projection  

↓

Quantitative operators (ART layer):

addition  
multiplication / scaling  

↓

Domain-specific mathematical operators:

derivatives  
integrals  
tensor constructions  
probability operators

---

# 5. Advantages of the Two-Layer Basis

This separation provides several conceptual benefits.

1. **Structural minimality**

The ARW core remains independent of specific mathematical
representations such as vector spaces or metric spaces.

2. **Clear scope boundaries**

Mathematical scope can be defined relative to the closure
of the primitive operator set.

3. **Flexible instantiation**

ART models can introduce additional operators as required
without modifying the structural ARW layer.

4. **Unified BC interpretation**

Boundary conditions can be understood as operator chains
generated from the minimal structural basis.

---

# 5a. The ε Tension: Where ARW Touches the Quantitative Layer

The canonical ARW scope tuple S = (B, Π, Δ, ε) contains a structural tension
that the two-layer basis makes visible.

ε is defined as a resolution threshold via:

d_Π(x, y) ≤ ε  →  x ~_S y

This presupposes a **distance function** d_Π on the observable space.
A distance function requires:

- subtraction (or a difference structure)
- a norm or absolute value (scaling to non-negative reals)
- the triangle inequality

These are quantitative structures — they live in the ART layer, not in the
structural ARW core.

**Consequence:** The empirical ARW scope S = (B, Π, Δ, ε) as currently defined
is not a purely structural object. It straddles the ARW/ART boundary:
B, Π, Δ are structural (definable with composition, product, projection),
but ε requires the quantitative layer.

### Two resolutions

**Option A — Redefine ε as an equivalence relation:**

Replace the metric condition with a purely structural one:

x ~_S y  iff  π(x) = π(y) for all π ∈ Π

under a chosen equivalence relation ~ on the observable space.

This removes the metric requirement. ε becomes a **granularity parameter**
on the quotient structure Π(X)/~ rather than a real-valued threshold.
The scope tuple becomes:

S = (B, Π, Δ, ~)

where ~ is a congruence relation on the image of Π.

This is a fully structural formulation. Quantitative ε then appears as a
**representation** of ~ when a metric is available — an ART instantiation
of a structural ARW concept.

**Option B — Accept ε as a boundary concept:**

Treat ε as the minimal quantitative structure required to make
regime partitions empirically testable. Under this view, the ARW/ART
boundary runs *through* the scope tuple: B, Π, Δ are ARW-structural,
ε is the first ART-quantitative element.

This makes ε the precise location where structural theory meets
measurement — a principled boundary rather than an inconsistency.

### Recommendation

Option B is more conservative and consistent with current ARW usage.
Option A is more elegant but requires reformulating all existing
scope specifications to use equivalence relations instead of ε-values.

The tension should be noted explicitly in `docs/glossary/scope.md`
as a known boundary issue, with Option A flagged as a possible
future reformulation.

# 6. Open Questions

**Q-OB1 — Is {composition, product, projection} minimal?**
Addressed partially in Section 1: projection may be derivable from product
in spaces with full product structure. The question reduces to whether
ARW requires spaces without such structure — if not, the basis is
{composition, product}.

**Q-OB2 — Does the two-operator basis {composition, product} suffice?**
This is the core minimality question. It is equivalent to asking whether
ARW is faithfully embeddable in a cartesian category (a category with
finite products and a terminal object). If yes, ARW has a well-known
mathematical foundation. If no, a third structural primitive is needed
and must be identified.

**Q-OB3 — Stochastic BCs without new primitives?**
Section 3a argues that Expectation is a metric-weighted projection on X × Ω,
requiring no new structural primitive. This needs formal verification:
does conditional expectation E[·|F] reduce to projection + quantitative
operators in all cases, or are there stochastic structures that require
genuinely new primitives?

**Q-OB4 — ε as equivalence relation (Option A)?**
Can all existing ScopeSpecs be reformulated using congruence relations ~
instead of real-valued ε, without loss of empirical content?
This would fully separate the structural and quantitative layers.
Testable on CASE-0001 through CASE-0003 by reformulating their ε conditions
as explicit equivalence relations and checking whether the partition results
are preserved.

**Q-OB5 — Derivational depth ordering of BC classes**
Section 2 proposes that static BCs correspond to finite composition depth
and dynamic BCs to infinite depth (limiting operations). Is this ordering
total, partial, or incomparable for some BC class pairs?
Does it coincide with the state/dynamic scope stratification from
`docs/advanced/bc_stratification_dynamic_subscopes.md`?

---

# 7. Outlook

If the proposed basis is sufficient, ARW could be formulated as a
structural theory based on only three — or possibly two — primitive operators.

Quantitative mathematics would then appear as an **instantiation layer**
rather than a foundational requirement.

This would provide a clear separation between:

- structural regime theory (ARW): composition, product, projection
- quantitative model realization (ART): addition, multiplication, domain operators
- stochastic instantiation: product(Ω) + projection, no new structural primitives

**The deepest implication** is that ARW, if reducible to
{composition, product construction}, is a theory expressible in
any cartesian category — making it domain-neutral not just by
design intent but by mathematical necessity.

Regime partitions, BC classes, scope tuples, and transfer metrics
would all be expressible as categorical constructions, and the
ARW framework would inherit the full apparatus of category theory
as a structural foundation.

This remains a research program, not a result. The immediate next steps are:

1. Formally test Q-OB2: embed the six BC classes in a cartesian category
   and verify that all operator chains are representable.
2. Resolve the ε tension (Section 5a): decide between Option A and Option B
   and update `docs/glossary/scope.md` accordingly.
3. Verify Q-OB3: check whether stochastic BCs in CASE-0005
   require new structural primitives beyond product(Ω) + projection.
